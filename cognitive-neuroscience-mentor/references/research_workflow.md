# Research Workflow

## Research Question Discipline

Use FINER:

- Feasible: sample size, task, EEG quality, behavioral measures, analyzable conditions.
- Interesting: theoretical or applied relevance.
- Novel: contribution beyond existing ERP, microstate, or behavioral literature.
- Ethical: appropriate child-data use and non-stigmatizing risk interpretation.
- Relevant: contribution to cognitive neuroscience, developmental science, learning difficulties, or education.

For vague topics, ask a few focused questions and converge on one or more answerable RQs.

## Literature Search Standard

Use external search when:

- local literature does not match the population, task, measure, or risk group;
- the question involves a mechanism not covered locally;
- novelty, publishability, or current consensus matters;
- manuscript-ready citations are needed;
- the local evidence is adult-only, clinical-only, old, indirect, or methodologically mismatched.

Search for:

- supporting evidence;
- conflicting evidence;
- boundary conditions;
- methodological standards;
- population-specific evidence.

Prefer:

- systematic reviews and meta-analyses;
- peer-reviewed empirical studies;
- established methods papers;
- publisher pages, PubMed, Crossref, Semantic Scholar, Google Scholar, and official journal pages.

## Evidence Grading

Use these labels:

- Direct support: matching population, task, neural measure, and risk group.
- Partial support: matching mechanism but different task, sample, or measure.
- Background only: useful for framing but not for a specific result claim.
- Contradictory: evidence points against the proposed interpretation.
- Unverified: not yet checked against a reliable bibliographic source or original paper.

Never recommend an unverified citation for final manuscript use.

## Synthesis Standard

Synthesize by mechanism, not by listing papers.

Useful axes:

- inhibitory control demand;
- response inhibition;
- conflict monitoring;
- attention allocation;
- executive function;
- P3/P300 context updating or resource allocation;
- N2-related conflict or inhibition processes;
- ERP microstate timing and topography;
- child development and maturation;
- shared vs unique learning-risk profiles.

Every synthesis should include:

- convergent evidence;
- conflicting evidence;
- population/task differences;
- methodological limitations;
- what can be claimed;
- what would be overclaiming.

## Devil's Advocate Check

Before accepting an interpretation, ask:

- Could another cognitive mechanism explain the result?
- Could preprocessing, trial count, baseline, artifact rejection, or group imbalance explain it?
- Does the task isolate inhibitory control, or also attention, speed, working memory, motivation, or language/math familiarity?
- Is the microstate label being interpreted too literally?
- Is the timing consistent with the proposed ERP process?
- Would a reviewer ask for behavioral correlations, robustness checks, topographic validation, or correction for multiple comparisons?
