# Manuscript And Review Guide

## Argument Structure

A strong paper should make the chain explicit:

1. Early learning and attention risks are linked to executive control.
2. Inhibitory control can be probed with Go/No-Go or related tasks, but task impurity must be acknowledged.
3. Traditional ERP components reveal time-locked neural responses.
4. ERP microstates add information about topographic temporal dynamics.
5. Risk groups may show shared and unique temporal dynamics.
6. Findings should be interpreted in relation to behavior and developmental constraints.

For non-ERP or non-developmental papers, use the same logic in generalized form:

1. What unresolved cognitive/neural problem exists?
2. Why does the chosen method address it?
3. What does the user's evidence show?
4. What mechanism is supported, and what mechanism is only speculative?
5. What boundary prevents overclaiming?
6. What does the study add to the field?

## Introduction

Check whether the Introduction:

- explains why inhibitory control matters for early school-age children;
- distinguishes attention, reading, and mathematics risk;
- justifies ERP microstates beyond traditional ERP components;
- identifies a clear gap;
- states specific hypotheses.

## Results

Results should:

- report direction, uncertainty, and effect size;
- avoid storytelling based only on p-values;
- make condition, group, time window, and metric clear;
- separate planned and exploratory analyses.

## Discussion

Discussion should:

- start from the strongest result;
- connect findings to specific literature;
- include alternative explanations;
- avoid causal overreach;
- address developmental variability;
- acknowledge method limits;
- state what the study adds.

## Reviewer Risk Checklist

Expect reviewers to ask:

- Are risk groups well-defined and mutually exclusive?
- Are trial counts adequate and balanced?
- Are microstate parameters justified?
- Are maps comparable across groups and conditions?
- Are multiple comparisons controlled?
- Is the cognitive interpretation overextended?
- Are behavior and neural findings aligned?
- Are clinical labels used carefully?

## Wording Principles

Prefer:

- "may reflect"
- "is consistent with"
- "suggests a possible"
- "provides indirect evidence for"
- "should be interpreted cautiously"

Avoid:

- "proves"
- "demonstrates the neural mechanism of"
- "is a biomarker of" unless validated independently
- "ADHD/dyslexia/dyscalculia" for non-diagnostic risk groups unless the sample is clinically diagnosed
